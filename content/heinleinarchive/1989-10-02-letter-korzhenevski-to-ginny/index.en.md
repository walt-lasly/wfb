# Letter from Alexander Korzhenevski to Ginny Heinlein (Tunnel in the Sky)

*Alexander Korzhenevski who has just finished translating Tunnel in the Sky for the first Russian Heinlein book (Moscow Polygraphic Institute). He asks Ginny's permission to also include RAH's James Forrestal Memorial Lecture (Analog, January 1974). He closes with a P.S. asking for a recent publisher-quality photo of RAH — he only has the British NEL paperback covers.*

<p class="letter-date">October 2, 1989</p>

Dear Mrs. Heinlein,

Thank you for the letter of July 9.

The "Grumbles", as you describe it, will be very impressive and I'll be looking forward to seeing it (May I hope that you'll sign the book for me?)

I've finished translating "Tunnel in the Sky" two days ago, and it was real pleasure to translate.

The editors also asked me to prepare biographical note, bibliography of Russian translations and were very interested when I told them about an article by Mr. Heinlein I've found in January 74 issue of "Analog" magazine — it's James Forrestal Memorial Lecture at Naval Academy at Annapolis delivered on April 5, 1973, which "Analog" used as Guest Editorial. I feel it's really important and I'd gladly translate it to include into the book, but there's a problem.

Soviet Union signed the Universal Copyright Convention on May 27, 1973, and so anything copyrighted after this day can be published only with author's (or estate's, or agent's) permission here. While publishers do publish "pre-convention" works without arrangements, they have to obtain permission for "after-convention" items. So I've been asked to write to you to ask if maybe you'll agree that this is a good idea — to publish this article (it's going to be the first real book by Robert Heinlein here, and I feel it'd be great to let the readers see this "Heinlein in his own words") — and will give your consent to this publication (one-time-use only for publishing house of Moscow Polygraphic Institute)? Please, let me know your decision in any case. I think (in case it's positive), your written permission will be enough for publishers. Oh, I should mention that this publishing house isn't capable of making payment in convertible currency, only in Soviet roubles if that's necessary (which I think in this case simply isn't worth it). So

I'll be looking forward to hearing from you, and hoping--

Sincerely, Alexander Korzhenevski
{{< figure src="signature_alex_k.png" alt="Fred" caption="Alex" >}}

*P.S.* Could you, please, send me some recent photo of Mr. Heinlein which publishers can use? (I have only those on the back covers of British NEL paperbacks, and they aren't very good)

{{< /handwritten >}}

**Notes**

**Moscow Polygraphic Institute** — A publisher affiliated with the Moscow State University of Printing Arts. In the glasnost era (1989) it was among the first Soviet houses to produce legitimate, licensed Heinlein editions rather than unauthorized reprints.

***Tunnel in the Sky* (1955)** — RAH's novel about students stranded on an alien planet when a survival-test portal fails to reopen. It was a favorite among Soviet readers interested in SF, with earlier samizdat (unofficial) translations circulating for years before Korzhenevski's authorized version.

**Universal Copyright Convention** — The UCC, to which the Soviet Union acceded on May 27, 1973. Before that date, Soviet publishers routinely printed foreign works without permission or payment. Afterwards, Korzhenevski needed Ginny's written consent for any post-May 1973 text — hence the formal request.

**James Forrestal Memorial Lecture** — Delivered by RAH at the US Naval Academy, Annapolis, on April 5, 1973; published as a guest editorial in *Analog Science Fiction*, January 1974. James Forrestal (1892–1949) was the first US Secretary of Defense.

***Grumbles from the Grave*** — RAH's posthumous collection of letters, edited by Ginny and published by Del Rey Books in November 1989 — only weeks after this letter was written. Korzhenevski had apparently heard about it from Ginny's earlier correspondence.

**NEL paperbacks** — New English Library (London) published several RAH titles in the 1970s–80s with photographic cover portraits; these were the only pictures of RAH readily available to Soviet correspondents.
