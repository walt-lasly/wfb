# Letter from Philip José Farmer to RAH (redheads research and Stranger)

*Farmer writes from Scottsdale, January 1962. He is studying Navajo morphemics at ASU, defends "Flesh" as inspired by Graves' "The White Goddess", delivers a long redheads dossier for RAH's planned fantasy, praises "Stranger in a Strange Land", and reports selling "Vashti" to Regency.*

{{< typewriter >}}

<p class="letter-date">January 1962</p>

{{< figure src="id.png" >}}

Dear Bob:

I'm starting the New Year off by answering most of my important correspondence. This week and the following offer me my last chance for much free time, not much really but more than usual. Besides having to work much overtime on a book at Motorola, I've also been exercising my eyes every night with prisms in an effort to correct an increasing exotropism of the eyes, a muscular imbalance. Now, the many many nights spent at the exercises are beginning to pay off, the eyeaches and headaches and fatigue are going away, and I don't even use my glasses any more, haven't worn them for two months. However, the gain of one thing quite often means the loss of another. I have little trouble in reading now but can't see so clearly the objects a great distance off.

One of the reasons I won't have too much time, starting Feb. 1, is that I'll be taking six hours of graduate work in English, plus working 40 hours a week, plus writing in the evenings, plus whatever else might come up, and it always does. However, one of the classes will be very interesting, it's Morphemics (which I'm taking as one of my Anthropology minors). We'll be analyzing, or trying to analyze, the morphemic structure (and phonemic) of Navaho as spoken by a man (or woman) imported for this purpose. Although this class has been introduced by the English department at ASU, it is also an anthropology course, and the Anthropology department hopes that the better students will be able eventually to go to the reservation and do linguistic research (on a limited basis, of course). I'm looking forward to it, although the fact that I had to skip the Phonemics prerequisite last semester (due to the eye trouble) will handicap me for a while. But, since I'm older and have done a certain amount of independent study in linguistics, the professor feels I can handle it. Doctor Lamberts himself is peculiarly qualified to conduct this class. He had two Ph.D's, one in English and one in Linguistics. He's a very good and very undogmatic teacher, as I learned when I took his class in Structure of American English semester before last. This class was an eye-opener in many ways; it's amazing how many things you don't know about something you've lived with night and day and practiced for 42 years. Makes you live in a new universe.

You said you were starting on FLESH. Accept my explanations about certain jarring aspects of that book; the editor cut it senselessly, sometimes in the middle of a paragraph or sentence. However, I had fun writing it, I did have a free hand (to a point) in writing it, and, as you point out, it takes the attitude that what we regard as tabu or obscene in this culture may be quite the reverse in others. Main inspiration for the book came after reading my hero Robert Graves' THE WHITE GODDESS. I would have sent him a copy except for two things: the embarrassing blurbs on the covers, the botching the editor did, and, a third reason that just came to me, the writing, which would not be up to Graves' standards. Still, he might have gotten a kick out of it. I read recently that Graves has left the island of Majorca and accepted the chair of Poetry at Oxford for five years. Wouldn't it be something to take a course under him?

<p class="section-break">✲ ✲ ✲</p>

About your fantasy-to-be on THE RED-HEADED RACE. No, I don't have any such idea for a story. Yours sounds interesting; I hope I won't have to wait too long to read it. Alas, I do not remember the various sources for my statement that Genghis Khan, Alexander, etc. were redheads. I could make an effort to look them up. But I have read that they were auburn-haired in various books dealing with their lives. More than one book has stated that Genghis Khan had red hair, I think Lamberts' book said so. Most books about Judas mention his red hair, the notion was common in medieval folklore and led to some of the prejudices against redheads (although the same people who shared had this prejudice overlooked the same lore about Jesus being a redhead, but such is prejudice). Who wrote the book about Byron? Drinkwater, I think. Don't remember now which of several books stated that King David had red hair. Speaking of other famous redheads, Marshal Ney (Napoleon's marshal) had red hair. The great nineteenth-century explorer and linguist, Sir Richard Francis Burton, had red hair and blue eyes until the age of three, then, almost overnight, they changed to jetblack hair and dark brown eyes. Could you fantasize about this strange conversion, use it in your story? Didn't Robespierre or Danton have red hair? One of the great French revolutionists did. And one of the early Norman kings of England, Rufus, I think. His story is in THE GOD OF THE WITCHES by Margaret Murray, a fascinating book. Richard the Lion-Hearted (?) Of him I'm not at all sure but it would bear looking up. Achilles is another whose color of hair could be quickly determined by a look at the Iliad. Of course, I don't know if you're interested only in historical characters. If you want mythological types, then Loki was redhaired. There has been some speculation that Loki's redhairedness was transferred to Judas by the loremakers. And I'm trying to recall the friend or relative of Mohammed who was redhaired but can't. Ben Jonson, as I remember it, was redhaired. One of his eyes was located higher than the other. Don't know if that latter aspect will cause you to fantasize. In Melanesian mythology two redheaded heroes, Gwau Meo and Sina Kwao, figure prominently. (See the Standard Dictionary of Folklore, Mythology, and Legend, Funk & Wagnalls.) Also, don't forget George Washington.

The above exhausts, for the moment, my recollections of redheads. However, I'll see if I can locate my sources.

Yes, I enjoyed STRANGER. More, was thrilled by it. And predicted what the reaction of the majority of so-called flexible-minded s-f readers would be. Rightly, I think, judging from the reviews I've read so far. Oh, the rationalizations for condemning the latter part of the book are seemingly logically based, but they're rationalizations. The readers were repulsed, managed to ignore other-cultural explanations (just picked this up again after a week and a half), just couldn't stand the idea of human beings, especially the protagonist, behaving in such a manner, and, even stronger factor, were shocked that you, Robert Heinlein, would write such a novel.

Well, I'm glad you did for more than one reason. In the first place, it demonstrated that you're master at just about any type of plot or premise you adopt. Second, it may have caused some of your readers to think. Think, not rationalize. Third, it showed that you're not the conservative that Bester considered you to be in that critical article in MoF&SF and was especially effective in appearing hard on the heels of the article. Fourth, those who thought they had you pegged will be wondering, what next?

Now, some of the criticisms made were that the book broke in half, that the converts to the New Religion were always protesting overmuch how happy they were with their new way of life (some of these criticisms did not appear in reviews but were made by some s-f LA fans I talked to when they came through Scottsdale), that the protagonist hypnotized his converts and forced them against their will to be converted, and so on.

Personally, I didn't think the book broke in half; the first half contained elements and clues that should not have startled the reader on coming to the second. You had laid the groundwork for it. But, of course, the reader habituated to your books had never seen you take off on sex before, did not expect it, expected something else (he didn't know what but never this) and was so upset that he did not see that the first half could lead, very logically, to the second. Perhaps, if some author known for treating sex had written it, he might have been conditioned to expect it. But you pulled the rug from under him. And a good thing, too. Now, he can't ever be sure that you're not going to bowl him over again, that he can be comfortable. Although I would have thought that, after STARSHIP TROOPERS, he would have known better than relax.

By the above, I don't mean that you were predictable, that one reading your books wouldn't be continually surprised by novelty. I mean that it was the particular field you chose to develop that upset the reader. And, of course, your ideas while developing it.

For instance, your turning pornography and ecdysiasm over and examining possible benefits and natural traits in it. That, I think, startled and angered the reader (not me, our hypothetical s-f reader well acquainted with Heinlein).

Another thing you did was to dive deep below the social man (the man conditioned to behave as society thinks he should) to the natural man. You dived and struck bottom, plowed into the muddy bottom, and threw up some clouds and, also, some glowing starfish. You put on paper the natural man, spread some of the primal stuff of sex out before the reader, demonstrated that it was only logical that one who loves his fellow man should, inevitably, love and not discriminate. (And that was a good touch when showed that women felt the impact of the protagonist's kisses because he was totally concentrated in the act.)

Let's see. The protesting overmuch. I didn't notice it, but if it were true, then that's a touch of verity. It's well known that neo-converts spend much time discussing the happiness they derive from their religion, they want to share their joy.

As for the protagonist hypnotizing his converts, I didn't think of that at all when I read the book. Perhaps, I should read it again, but I can't soon because of my crowded schedule. But I doubt if he did. I got the impression he merely used his powers to open the way. Nobody was forced.

Of course, the book is a fantasy in the light of what we know of science today. These powers don't exist, as far as we know. And Earth beings could not overcome their jealousies unless they had the aid of a superbeing. With a superbeing, they could. However, the reader resents the idea that men and women might develop such a love if powers were available to them to do so. They resent being told that, given the way, here's how they might travel.

Before writing this letter, I read the pocketbook version of STARSHIP TROOPERS (had only read the magazine version before). I must apologize about some of the things I said about typical military organizations. Your military organization wasn't typical and could work as efficiently as you indicated. The basic premise I agree with, that is, that we are at war with Bugs (you name it, Communists, Fascists, what-have-you) and that we'd better realize it and get off our ass before they win.

Note: My new address is 7736 East Catalina Drive, Scottsdale. And I just sold a non-sf book to Regency paperbacks titled VASHTI. You might like it when it comes out in three or four months. It was turned down by some hardcover outfits who said it was likely to offend both liberals and conservatives. It is concerned with relations between Negroes and whites in a steel mill, partly based on my experiences in a steel mill (I worked 11 years in one), stresses the fact that Negroes not only suffer from the neuroses that whites have but also must carry the other burden of being Negro in North America, and says that much pressure and little light make for strange fish.

Best,

**Notes**

**Exotropism / exotropia** — Farmer's spelling; the standard medical term is *exotropia*, a form of strabismus in which one or both eyes deviate outward. He was correcting it with prismatic lenses used as exercises rather than full-time correction.

**Morphemics / ASU** — Arizona State University in Tempe. The class analyzed Navajo morphophonemic structure — directly relevant to the Sapir–Whorf hypothesis (linguistic relativity) that RAH asks about in his March 8 reply: "What are you learning about the internal structure of Navajo? Especially its implications in world picture, à la Whorf?"

**Doctor Lamberts** — Likely Harold Lamberts (or a variant spelling), who held doctorates in both English and Linguistics and taught at ASU. Farmer praises him as undogmatic — high praise from a writer who disliked academic conformity.

***Flesh* (1960)** — Farmer's Galaxy Novel, published under the title *Flesh* by Beacon Books. It depicts a future Earth with an elaborate fertility religion; the editor's cuts were notorious among Farmer scholars. The novel was later restored and republished by Doubleday (1968).

**Robert Graves / *The White Goddess*** — Graves's 1948 study of the mythological and poetic tradition of the "white goddess" of birth, love, and death in pre-Christian European religion. Farmer cites it as his primary inspiration for *Flesh*. Graves (1895–1985) was indeed Professor of Poetry at Oxford 1961–1966.

**"The Red-Headed Race"** — RAH's planned fantasy, for which he had shared the premise (famous historical figures as redheads) with Farmer, who here returns extensive research. The project apparently never reached publication; this exchange is the only known trace of it.

**Sir Richard Francis Burton (1821–1890)** — The great explorer, linguist, and translator of the *Arabian Nights*. Farmer's claim about Burton's hair changing from red to black in early childhood does not appear in standard biographies; it may derive from a secondary folkloric source.

**Rufus / William II** — William II of England (r. 1087–1100), nicknamed "Rufus" (the Red) for his red face or hair. His story as a possible sacrificial king appears in Margaret Murray's *The God of the Witches* (1931), a widely influential (if now academically discredited) study of pre-Christian European ritual.

**"Ecdysiasm"** — From *ecdysis* (the shedding of skin, as in insects or crustaceans); coined as *ecdysiast* by H. L. Mencken for striptease performers. Farmer uses the noun form "ecdysiasm" for the practice itself, in the context of RAH's exploration of the social and cultural dimensions of the erotic in *Stranger*.

**Bester's critical article in MoF&SF** — Alfred Bester apparently published a piece in *The Magazine of Fantasy & Science Fiction* characterizing Heinlein as a conservative writer. *Stranger in a Strange Land* (1961) confounded that reading, appearing, as Farmer notes, "hard on the heels of the article."

***Vashti*** — Farmer's novel about racial conflict in a steel mill, based on his eleven years working in one (at Peoria, Illinois). Sold to Regency Books (where AJ Budrys was now editor-in-chief), it appeared in 1962. RAH mentions wanting to read it in his March 8 reply, planning to obtain it through Budrys.
