# Letter from RAH to Philip José Farmer (Stranger and SF orthodoxy)

*RAH writes to Phil Farmer from Colorado Springs on March 8, 1962. He grants Farmer free use of the "redheads" gimmick they discussed. He asks about Farmer's novel A Woman a Day and hopes to get VASHTI through Algis Budrys. He admires Farmer's school work on the structure of English and Navajo (à la Whorf). And he reflects at length on the sharply polarized reception of Stranger in a Strange Land, quoting his own prediction that "no society will permit one to attack its postulates with impunity" — and diagnosing SF orthodoxy as more stifling than the mainstream.*

{{< typewriter >}}

<p class="letter-date">8 March 1962</p>

Dear Phil,
{{< figure src="phil_farmer.png" >}}

Don't feel obligated to answer this until you feel like it, if at all. You are obviously extremely busy. Me, I answer letters, all but urgent business mail, about every quarter or longer — or I get no work done. I am now between stories and trying to clear my desk so that I can start another one.

Thanks for the data re redheads and the green light to use same — and if you happen to change your mind and want to do a story with this gimmick, do go ahead. There won't be any conflict.

I shall look forward to VASHTI although I may have to ask Algis to send it to me. This is a small town and I live outside it and rarely go down town; I miss a lot of news stand items. Didn't you do a book called A WOMAN A DAY? Who published it and when? I missed it.

Your school work sounds delightful — and I do wish I could have taken that course on the structure of the English language. It is a subject I know little about but am fascinated by. What are you learning about the internal structure of Navajo? Especially its implications in world picture, à la Whorf?

How the hell you manage to go to school, work at a full job, head a family, and find time to write as much fiction, or more, as I do, defeats me. Some people (you, Harry Stine, Asimov, Arthur George Smith) I suspect of owning time manipulators.

Thanks for the very nice words about STRANGER. The response to it does seem to be either black, or white, with no gray — which is about what I had expected save that the favorable response is more numerous than I had guessed it would be . . . and the cries of rage from some of the reviewers is stronger than I had anticipated. Somewhere in the book I predicted the response in words somewhat like these: "No society will permit one to attack its postulates with impunity." Sex, religion, and politics are very, very touchy subjects in this culture; an author is expected to view with alarm and point with pride in certain stylized ways if he mentions these matters at all. If he doesn't, why, lynch the bastard!

For some reason not clear to me these bounds of orthodoxy are even more stringent in the SF&F field than in the so-called "mainstream." Some reviewers seem to feel that SF must never take itself seriously and any effort to write anything but BEMs & spaceships must be stamped out. Others appear to allow a little more leeway but in fact insist on an unstated orthodoxy which is vaguely humanist, agnostic, socialist, democratic, and pacifist. (I am not at the moment declaring either for or against any of those ideas; I just don't think that a reviewer should demand a "Ja!" vote.) As a result of this orthodoxy, SF is getting to be (or "is") pretty damned dull — except for a rare and heart-warming rebel, such as yourself. And, I hope, myself, for I hope to offend a lot of stuffed shirts before I quit.

Warmest good wishes,

{{< figure src="signature_rah.png" alt="Bob" caption="Bob" >}}

{{< /typewriter >}}

**Notes**

**Redheads gimmick** — RAH's letter implies he and Farmer had previously exchanged ideas and RAH shared a story concept involving redheads that Farmer was free to use.

**VASHTI** — a small-circulation SF bulletin/fanzine associated with the Midwest circuit; RAH lived outside Colorado Springs and missed many newstand items.

**A WOMAN A DAY** — Farmer's novel, first published in 1960 by Beacon Books under the title *Timestop!*; later reissued under its original title.

**Harry Stine** — G. Harry Stine (pen name Lee Correy), author and rocket engineer who, like Farmer and Asimov, maintained a prodigious output alongside a demanding day job.

**Arthur George Smith** — Arthur George "Sarge" Smith was a close friend, mentor, and significant influence on Robert A. Heinlein, described by Heinlein as one of the primary inspirations for the "wise old man" characters in his fiction.

**BEMs** — "Bug-Eyed Monsters," golden-age SF slang for the clichéd alien creatures that defined pulp-era science fiction in the eyes of its critics.

**"Ja!" vote** — an allusion to the Nazi-era plebiscites in which the only acceptable public answer was *Ja* (yes); RAH uses it to characterize reviewers who demand authors endorse the approved ideological line.
