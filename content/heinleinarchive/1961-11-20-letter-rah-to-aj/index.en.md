# Letter from RAH to Algis Budrys (bomb shelter)

*RAH responds to AJ's November 2 letter. He explains privately why he opposed AJ's intelligence career plan — fearing either a frustrating desk job or death in a covert Baltic operation. He reflects on the Cold War, the Bay of Pigs, and his willingness to die for freedom, but not cheaply. He describes spending six weeks dynamiting a bomb shelter into the granite under his Colorado Springs house, and urges AJ to build fallout protection too. On AJ's book proposal, RAH demurs but recommends Cyril Kornbluth's "Not This August" (1955) as an already-existing "Red America" novel. He mentions he was unable to attend the Pohls' farewell party for AJ.*

<p class="letter-date">20 Nov 1961</p>

Dear Algis,

I addressed an envelope for note paper but, after rereading your letter, I see that I will need more room--

Pix from Vilnius for your father: they are not yet copied, and I am so snowed under with work that it may be some time before I get around to it. But it will be done.

Your new job and the crossing of the Rubicon that went with it: I'm glad you did it. I am not disposed to try to run other people's lives and I wanted to be helpful when you were considering another major course of action. But, privately, I did not favor it. If it had worked out--if you were taken on for some sort of an intelligence assignment which involved your very special knowledge and background--I could see two major possibilities. The first was that you would be assigned to a desk job in Washington or in Europe, working at intelligence data evaluation or at writing copy for Radio Free Europe--and endlessly frustrated by decisions from a boss who believed that he knew more about the Baltic countries than you do even though he had never been there, or (worse yet) supervised by a jerk who was merely parlor pink instead of flaming red. The other possibility was that you might be sent, under a cover name and with all the cloak & dagger gear, to attempt contact with the Lithuanian underground--and probably eventually get scragged by the Commies.

Believe me, I feel sure that you would not hesitate to risk your life, including a messy and slow death--but no healthy man is anxious to play Russian roulette with his life. He will decide to do so when the circumstances merit it... decide in a split second if it is his own child who must be rescued. But he doesn't do it as a game and he doesn't think of it as "fun." I think you are a healthy and sane man; I do not suspect you of having even a touch of the death-wish bravado of your lead character in ROGUE MOON.

The thing that bothered me about the second possibility, that you might wind up unpleasantly dead, was that I was not sure it was worth it--a married man with kids. I do not mean that it would not be worth the price to rid the world of the bloody masters of the Kremlin; the total problem is worth your life and mine and that of many, many more--and I expect it to cost the lives of many millions before this planet is free again; I see no possibility of cheap victory.

But I had my doubts about this particular piece of the job... because I remember Budapest... and the Bay of Pigs in Cuba. I think that the United States should stir up rebellion behind the Iron Curtain everywhere and then give it all-out support and let the situation go straight into World War III if needed.

But for us to encourage rebellion and then fail to support it is both criminal and foolish. I see no disposition on the part of the present administration to grasp this nettle and do what is necessary--and I think the Bay of Pigs is perhaps the most shameful episode in our national history. I would not wish to see you throw away your life inciting a Baltic "Bay of Pigs."

Perhaps someday we Americans will face up to our task (or be forced, volle nolle, to face up to it)--in which case there will be work for you and for me, too, and for everyone of manhood and womanhood, and many of us will die in it. I anticipate this day with eagerness but not with pleasure. I have no wish to die slowly of radiation poisoning, nor from a bayonet in my guts, nor coughing my lungs out somewhere in the hills back of here as an overage and not too effective underground resistance fighter--by preference I would choose to die at a fairly advanced age, still sound of wind and limb and memory and zest, in a moment of euphoria by means of plane or car crash, or some other quick and unexpected guillotining.

But one's own mode of death is not a fully free choice; I expect that the future holds ample opportunity for all of us to die heroic but quite messy deaths. I estimate myself to be a reluctant hero at best; I think I have been tested enough in the past to believe, or hope, that I may play a manly part when the test comes. But I don't have to enjoy it.

As for yourself, I think you have made a wise choice. No matter how your passport reads America is your foster mother. You are the father of three native citizens--four in January--and the spouse of a fifth; your commitments here are stronger than mine, since I have no children. Since you can't control foreign policy, your name not being Kennedy, and at best can have only a microscopic influence on it, it seems to me that you are wise to cultivate your own garden--and serve in the future as circumstances permit.

Although committed to your foster country, it may be that you will choose to retain your native citizenship--and I for one would fully understand this, especially as your father is still living. But when and if you choose to become an American citizen, I want to welcome you with open arms--and I hope I will be able to come to the celebration. For all her faults, and I am painfully aware of them, I still think the United States of America is the greatest single achievment of the human race. There are other, little countries (such as Denmark, or Switzerland) which can be cited as great social achievments of h. sapiens--but 180,000,000 is more difficult, much more difficult.

I have spent the last six weeks taking my own advice, i.e., building a bomb shelter. I've put this off for ten years simply because it was confoundedly difficult and expensive to build one in connection with our house--and besides, we had no children. But my talk had some effect on me whether it moved the audience or not; I decided when we got home that it was silly and a touch hypocritical for me to talk to other people about how Americans must be prepared to resist, even if we lost, when I had made no preparations to survive an attack myself.

So I did it, starting about October 1st and it took us to the middle of this month. Our house has no basement, no attic, very large expanses of glass, and is about as transparent to radiation as a tent and about as vulnerable to blast as a house can be. It is a very nice house to live in, but utterly worthless as a shelter.

So we had to dig one outside and this was not easy, as we sit on a solid granite shelf--another reason why I had procrastinated. But dig we did, with a great deal of dynamiting, and now it is finished at last and adequately stocked. No, I don't expect the fat boy to try his chances with us this winter (although I know of others who are much better informed than I am who do expect war almost at once); I am planning this for the next twenty years, for the rest of my life. In the mean time Ginny is learning to shoot a rifle and seems to have a natural talent for it.

May I urge you to do something along this line, too? Fallout protection, I mean; if you and your family live in Evanston or north of it, I rather doubt if you will need blast protection except against a missile gone astray--which is something nobody can plan against. But, so far as I know, there is nothing within twenty miles of you and probably much farther which justifies the Kremlin including it in their list of first-strike targets. (Perhaps I'm mistaken; there may be a SAC base or an Atlas installation or something else that they must hit practically in your lap--as we have NORAD in our lap. But I don't think so--better check a bit.)

If it is just for fallout and fire protection, a Dr. Libby 333 shelter would keep your wife and kids, and yourself, alive through an all-out attack and the waiting period afterwards--if you have a strong back, a shovel, and a Sears-Roebuck wheelbarrow. I hope you do it--that's a nice bunch of kids.

<p class="section-break">✲ ✲ ✲</p>

Speaking of such (and thanks very much for the pix), don't gimme that guff about how carefully you chose your spouse for genetic reasons! She's a beautiful gal, you fell in love with her, you persuaded her to marry you, period. And congratulations! But don't ask me to believe that you picked her out with cold cerebration as one might pick out a new refrigerator; I won't believe it. She is a lovely creature. Perhaps she is a fine cook as well--but I'll bet such side issues had no relevance to your choice.

I look forward to the privilege of meeting her.

Business: I appreciate the suggestion that I might do a book for Regency re life in a Communist America, or a related theme. I have given this much thought and must give it much more thought before I could possibly answer affirmatively. Such a book should be a strong and effective piece of propaganda, or it should not be published--and I am not sure of my ability to make it so. I want to write it--but I have not been markedly successful with non-fiction in the past. It might turn out to be a dreary and ineffective sermon.

Trying to tell people who don't know the nature of communism through their own experience is a bit like trying to explain the meaning of sex to a virgin.

Possibly I will tackle it someday in fiction form. In the mean time here is a suggestion. There is already in existence a very strong and very competent novel about "Red America" published six years ago, which received only limited distribution, and is now probably out of print. In my opinion it is much more timely now than when it was published. I refer to Cyril Kornbluth's NOT THIS AUGUST.

Here is a book that could be blurbed as "This Is What the Berlin Crisis Means to Every Man, Woman, and Child in America!"

I don't know whether the book ever appeared as a pocketbook. Even if it did, all rights have probably reverted to Mary Kornbluth by now--or, if not, the pocketbook publisher should either be willing to resign all claim or promptly bring out a new edition. I suggest that you read it, or reread it, and see what you think of the idea.

I'm sorry I was not able to attend the farewell party given by the Pohls in your honor. And I shall most certainly look you all up the next time I am in Chicago with or without being "royally entertained at Regency's expense." By the way, is Regency a Hamling enterprise? Whether it is or it isn't, please give Bill and Frances my warm regards when you see them--I met them some years ago and found them both very charming.

It is late at night and I must stop this and do a couple of chores before I sack in.

All the best,

**Notes**

**Bay of Pigs** — The CIA-backed invasion of Cuba by Cuban exile forces, April 17–20, 1961 — a humiliating failure for the Kennedy administration. RAH cites it alongside Budapest as evidence that the US encourages resistance behind the Iron Curtain and then abandons it.

**Budapest 1956** — The Hungarian Revolution (October–November 1956), crushed by Soviet tanks after Radio Free Europe had been implicitly promising Western support. RAH keeps this in mind when assessing whether AJ's potential covert assignment to the Baltic underground would receive real US backing.

***Rogue Moon*** — AJ's acclaimed 1960 novel; its protagonist has an almost suicidal fearlessness about death. RAH explicitly distances AJ from this character trait.

**Dr. Libby shelter** — Willard F. Libby, Nobel laureate chemist and former member of the Atomic Energy Commission, publicized a simple backyard fallout shelter in 1961 that could be built cheaply with a shovel and a wheelbarrow. It was widely promoted in civil defense materials.

**NORAD** — North American Aerospace Defense Command, headquartered at Ent Air Force Base in Colorado Springs — effectively RAH's backyard, making his home a near-certain first-strike target in any nuclear exchange.

***Not This August*** — C. M. Kornbluth's 1955 novel (also published as *Christmas Eve*) depicting a Soviet-occupied United States. RAH recommends it as the "Red America" book AJ wants him to write. Kornbluth died in 1958 at 34; his widow Mary held all rights.

**"volle nolle"** — RAH's rendering of the Latin *nolens volens* ("whether willing or unwilling," i.e., willy-nilly).

**Bill Hamling** — William L. Hamling, publisher of Regency Books (AJ's employer) and of the SF magazines *Imagination* and *Imaginative Tales*.
