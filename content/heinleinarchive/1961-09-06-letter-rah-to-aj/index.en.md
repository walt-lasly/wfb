# Letter from RAH to Algis Budrys

*RAH writes to AJ days after the Seattle Worldcon (Sep 2–4, 1961), where they met through Fred Pohl. Still ill from the fever he ran during his Guest of Honor speech, Heinlein offers to use his Pentagon connections to help Budrys pursue an intelligence career. He then pivots to a long reflection on Baltic history, the Terror, and the Vilnius incident involving Ginny. He also responds to AJ's recommendation of Robert Abernathy's "When the Rockets Come" (1945).*

_See also:_ <https://fanac.org/conpubs/Worldcon/Seacon/index.html>

<p class="letter-date">6 September 1961</p>



Dear Algis,

I am sending this to you care of Fred Pohl because I do not have your address—I felt sure that I did have it, that I had obtained it from you at Seattle... but I am unable to find it today. We left in great haste, Dr. Chris having told Ginny to get me home at once and put me to bed... which she did and I am somewhat better now. But in the rush several papers were lost or mislaid.

In re our private conversation: should you see fit to do that which you told me you are contemplating, let me now repeat in writing what I can do. I have no influence—I am sure that your distinguished father must be far better acquainted in Washington than I am. But I do know some people so situated that it might be worth while to by-pass the bureaucratic red tape in order to let them see a précis of your record and special qualifications.

This in itself would do nothing; it would simply mean that a very senior officer would glance at it, then send it to someone on his staff for evaluation—and all this means is that it enters the system at the top, rather than the bottom, speeds things up a bit and reduces the chance of your papers being lost in the shuffle. But if they are not in the market for left-handed monkey wrenches that day, you'll be turned down just the same.

By "very senior officer" I simply mean that I know socially some of those at the top—while I myself am nothing; I was disabled out and retired almost thirty years ago. But the Chief of Naval Operations was classmate of my next older brother and a friend; the Deputy Chief of Naval Operations is a classmate of mine, a friend who roomed across from me in USS Lexington no. 5—she who was sunk in the Coral Sea. The Chief of Staff of the Air Force is godfather to one of my nieces and a very old friend of my sister-in-law; the Deputy Chief of Staff is an old friend of both Ginny and myself, he married a daughter of my former skipper, the late Fleet Admiral King; Ginny and I have stayed with them in Japan and in Germany; my STARMAN JONES is dedicated to one of his sons.

This is what I mean by "socially"—as you can see, the relationships are not professional at all; I simply can reach them directly... and am trusted not to do so unless the occasion warrants it. In the case of the two Air Force generals I will see them in Philadelphia two weeks from now and, if there is opportunity, I may speak to one or the other of them privately—however, an Air Force convention is ten times as noisy and crowded as the one we just went through; the chances of private conversation are poor.

My connections in the Pentagon with the Army are not as good as with the Navy and the Air Force. One of my brothers is a retired general but so far as I know at the moment he is not acquainted with the present Chief of Staff. I know just one Army general now in the Pentagon; he and I were corporals in the same regiment and very close friends back in 1923—but I don't know what his job is and it may be quite unrelated to the work you are fitted for. But I could phone him if needed and then possibly he would know the man who would be interested in seeing that précis.

But, Algis, I do think you have some very unusual qualities, else I would take no interest in this at all...for, believe me, I do not bother high brass simply as a favor to some friend—these are not the 'twenties and the load on them is much too heavy for such nonsense. If I recall correctly, you know (or can polish up on) Russian, Lithuanian, High German, French, and, of course, English—and that you acquired your accent in these languages as a child rather than in your adult years—if so, this could be valuable under some circumstances. Running with this would be, I suspect, a countless number of little things. For example I would guess that you can use your fork with your left hand and hold your knife in the Continental manner without having to stop to think about it—exempli gratia of many, many more things. Plus, I hazard, knowledge of matters and channels which never get into the news.

Of course, if you do happen to be exactly the left-handed monkey wrench they are looking for, it also is likely to mean that you won't live very long... but I assume conclusively that you have already given this aspect full thought and I will say no more about it.

<p class="section-break">✲ ✲ ✲</p>

Being laid up ill anyhow today, I dug up and read, on your indirect recommendation "WHEN THE ROCKETS COME" by Robert Abernathy. Now I must get Ginny to read it and to translate the Russian in it; I don't know that much Russian and Russian looks odd in Roman letters anyhow.

It is indeed quite a story and now I must reread it carefully and think about it, both its philosophy and its technique. It says a number of things with which I agree, some of which I tried to say in STARSHIP TROOPERS—thereby infuriating some people, whereas this story apparently did not get anyone's goat even though the propositions are stated even more rawly and brutally than I stated them in TROOPERS. It's the difference in date I rather think; 1945 and 1960 are worlds apart in Zeitgeist.

I do not think that I have ever read this story before, for I do not think I could have forgotten it. This puzzled me until I considered the date—early 1945 and right smack in the middle of the Kamikaze, when we were suffering tremendous losses...unbearable losses had they kept up much longer. My own last ship got it on the picket line—USS Roper, I used to be her Gunnery Officer. My assistant G.O. got it about the same time, went down with his first command, fighting his ship until she sunk under him and won a posthumous Medal of Honor.

Me, I was safe enough, holding down a swivel chair—but working about fourteen hours a day on anti-Kamikaze, plus another project so hot that a report on it had to be on Fleet Admiral King's desk at 0800 every Monday morning. I was working so hard—and so upset by news that did not get into the papers—that I was under sedatives most of the time just to keep from blowing my stack. Ginny was on that project, too, and she was close to flipping her lid, too—she just missed buying herself a general court martial at one point. She was perfectly justified... but I'm afraid my little redhead just simply does not have "military" temperament—all women are anarchists but on her it shows more.

Anyhow, I probably put that issue of Astounding aside, saved it for later—and never did read it. Or didn't read it all, as I don't think I ever saw that fine job by Mr. Abernathy. Thanks.

And thanks for the help on my talk. I had written stuff about Lietuva into the draft without any clear intention of using it. It meant something to me—but I wasn't sure it would mean anything to most of my listeners; most people know so little geography and history. "Lithuania? That's that? A new detergent? Or an a foreign car?"—that was the response I sourly expected.

But only a day or two before the convention I had read somewhere that you were Lithuanian (instead of Polish, as someone once told me) and then I heard you being introduced (I had not known you were going to be there)—and grabbed you. I wanted to use that material and then did use it, because I had at least one person in the audience I knew would understand it. I could talk to him and hope that it would project over into the others. With your confirmation that I had my facts straight I could speak with confidence.

It is hard to talk to most people about such things, hard to put it over. Most Americans have never been outside their own country, most of them have never had any direct contact with war and violence—and as for understanding the Terror, hell, they honestly believe that what Senator McCarthy stirred up was a "Terror"—as if public embarrassment were in any way comparable to being taken out and shot or, worse, being sent to a slave camp!!! Hell, these parochial creampuffs understand as little about it as a virgin does about sex.

I don't know how many besides yourself I managed to reach. Not many, I'm afraid. Shucks, I don't really know what I said myself; I may have hung up a new records of sorts, it may have been the first time that the g. of h. address was given by a g. of h. running so high a fever that he didn't know what he was saying—once I almost fell off the platform, couldn't remember where I was in my remarks, and decided to chop it off short and get it over with. I'm going to have to get the tape and find out what I said.

But I did want to talk about Lietuva. Algis, I am the sort of person who cannot be detached—I get committed. In the short time I was there I got committed and I always will be. I feel the same way about Suomi and, to a somewhat lesser degree, about Latvija. An Vilnius will always be the place to me where, for the first time, I sensed what a political Terror meant.

You know the incident I mean—I heard Ginny telling you about it. Ginny is a committed person, too, always. She did something that night that pleased and touched me very much. After we had each memorized the message and the address and I then burned it, she embraced this frightened man, gently and tenderly, and kissed him on the cheek and told him soberly that he had nothing to fear from us.

So I hugged him too and then checked the corridor and he hurried away—maybe straight into the arms of the police, I don't know... that's the hell of it, you never can know, when a country is being run that way. But probably not, I assume—or hope—since the police did not bother us.

We have quite a number of pictures that Ginny took in Vilnius. I doubt if they have any intelligence value, but they might possibly have some sentimental interest. Either way, if you, or your father—or any properly interested person—wishes to see them, or to have them copied, we would be glad to send them to you. Ginny has told me she would be glad to, I mean. She uses them to lecture with, but she has no early dates coming up.

The news looks very bad tonight—which in my mind and, I think, in yours, is good news. That is to say that, grisly as the prospect is, I believe that the only hope for the free world and for the captive peoples lies in all-out war. This is not cheering to me, except in the most abstract and intellectual way, as, if they target in on NORAD, as they certainly can be expected to on their first strike, our home is practically at ground zero. But I don't see any other way out, no other that offers any prospect of freedom to the survivors.

I've run on too long. Ginny and I were warmly pleased to have met you and look forward to another time, Deus volent. You might, if you wish, do something for me. I asked to see pictures of your family and you did show them to me—but you recall what the circumstances were, convention press and noise. We would enjoy having pictures of the kids and Mrs. Budrys. Ginny and I lack children through no wish of our own; consequently we are more than usually interested in other people's children—it involves this "committedness" of which I spoke; other people's children tie us into the race, instead of being cut off from it by having none of our own. (The nicest thing that happened to me at the convention was meeting little Debbie Cornell—she gave me a cookie, somewhat chewed.)

And so goodnight... and with a prayer that your #4 will grow up in a free world. My respects to your wife and to your father, and our warm good wishes to you.

P.S. I have decided, after rereading this, that I must send it directly to you, rather than via anyone, no matter how close a friend. Surely, most of it could be published in a paper, but one or two items—well, on some subjects it is better to be overly cautious... and you have some tentative plans that are strictly your business.

**Notes**

**Seacon 1961** — The 19th World Science Fiction Convention, Seattle, August 31–September 4, 1961. RAH was Guest of Honor and delivered his celebrated address while running a high fever; he nearly collapsed on the platform.

**USS Lexington** — CV-2, sunk at the Battle of the Coral Sea (May 8, 1942). RAH's DCNO classmate had roomed across from him aboard her.

**USS Roper** — Wickes-class destroyer DD-147, on which RAH served as Gunnery Officer. He describes her taking a hit on the Kamikaze picket line in 1945 and praises his assistant Gunnery Officer, who went down with his first command and received a posthumous Medal of Honor.

**Fleet Admiral King** — Ernest J. King, Commander in Chief US Fleet and Chief of Naval Operations during WWII. RAH's former skipper; the letter implies Ginny knew his daughter's family.

**Anti-Kamikaze project** — RAH spent the last year of the war on classified work at the Philadelphia Naval Air Development Center (alongside Isaac Asimov and L. Sprague de Camp), producing weekly reports that went to King's desk every Monday morning.

**Robert Abernathy / "When the Rockets Come"** — A 1945 *Astounding* story by Abernathy depicting a future totalitarian Earth; RAH had apparently missed it on first publication because of the Kamikaze crisis. He relates it thematically to *Starship Troopers* (1959).

**The Vilnius incident** — RAH and Ginny traveled to the Soviet Union in the late 1950s. In Vilnius (then a Soviet city, now Lithuania's capital) a frightened stranger passed them a message, which they memorized and burned. The scene RAH describes — Ginny embracing the man, a check of the corridor, a hurried departure — is characteristic tradecraft caution.

**"All women are anarchists"** — A characteristic Heinlein aphorism, delivered here with obvious affection for Ginny.

**Debbie Cornell** — An unidentified child at the convention who gave RAH a partially chewed cookie; he cites it as the nicest thing that happened to him at Seacon.
