# Letter from Algis Budrys to RAH (with book proposal)

*AJ replies nearly two months after Seacon, explaining he has been swept up replacing Harlan Ellison as editor at Regency Books (effective October 1). He thanks RAH for his willingness to help and praises his GoH speech. He describes his wife Eddie and their three sons in vivid detail, and formally proposes that Heinlein write a political book for Regency — tentatively titled "Red America — Your Life If The Commies Win." He closes by mentioning a farewell party in his honor at Fred Pohl's home in Red Bank, NJ.*

<p class="letter-date">November 2, 1961</p>

Dear Bob:

Please forgive my not having replied to you in this long while. Immediately after the Seattle con, events began moving quite rapidly--and so did I. Details would bore you, but your letter followed me through four forwarding addresses while I was involved in the process that culminated in my replacing Harlan as Regency's editor. I took over the job--officially--on October 1, and since then have seen my wife and kids for a total of three hours. I'll be driving back East for five days the middle of this month, and sometime early in December I'll be moving my family out here, but meanwhile I'm practically living in this office, trying to get things into shape. Harlan is a nice guy, but an organizer and administrator he's not.

I imagine I've taken the big decision--this job is at least comparable in pay and fringe benefits to what I would earn exercising the political side of my talents, and I imagine that unless it gets down to open warfare, openly acknowledged, I'm henceforth a professional editorial consultant. I love the work in general, and, specifically, Regency is so constituted as to let me produce books that will do something more than just entertain.

But please accept my gratitude for your willingness to help--and let me also express my admiration for your stamina; you were a magnificent host. I trust you're now completely over your virus. Under the circumstances that obtained in Seattle--in fact, under any circumstances--all I can say to describe your charm under adversity is that you would be an ornament to any diplomatic corps.

And I was very much moved by your speech--having read parts of it ahead of time, I was nevertheless very much moved to hear them spoken.

Thank you very much for the offer of the Vilnius photographs. I'm sure my father would appreciate having them. At the moment, he's just out of the hospital, having suffered a coronary during New York's last heatwave of the season, and something like this--something fresh to attract his interest, and yet something he can work with in the relaxed conditions of his own home--would be just about the right size of ticket. If you'll send them to me at this address, I'll forward them. I've written to him about your trip, and about your speech, but haven't had a chance to discuss it with him in person.

<p class="section-break">✲ ✲ ✲</p>

And, as you can see, I've enclosed pictures of my gang of Chinese bandits. The little one--who looks like an Irishman--is, by fortunate coincidence, Tim; the middle one with the curls and the impish heart is Steve; and the big one with the slanted brown eyes and the glasses is Jeff--he's the acrobat of the family, and neighborhood champion tree-climber. Scarface, we call him. (The glasses are shatterproof--they have to be.) Heredity has been working overtime with this crew; Eddie, my wife, is Irish, French, Italian Swiss, and Austrian--my best guess is that a Hun got into the woodpile somewhere, because both she and her father would look more at home in Khazakstan than they would in Dublin. So would Jeff, if it weren't for his blonde hair. Stevie, on the other hand, is a dumpling with big blue eyes and a personality to match--introverted, and full of hell. Tim has big blue eyes, too--and an Irish temper to go with his black hair, which is several shades darker than that of any of his immediate ancestors. I--I hope you understand everything I mean by this--picked their mother very carefully, figuring it was a very good time to get some hybrid diversification and toughness into the line, but the results have exceeded all expectations. The next one arrives in January, and I can hardly wait to see what Mendel has wrought now.

Decent pictures of my wife are scarce. I've always tended to shy away from photographs--I don't know of more than a dozen pictures of me, baby portraits aside, though of course others have been taken at public occasions--and if my wife didn't feel differently, snapping a shutter at the kids on the slightest excuse, I wouldn't even have these. But it would be I who took the pictures of Eddie, and the result is that there aren't many, and only one is any good. That one ('good' being a relative term) was taken when she was markedly pregnant, in 1960. It tends to exaggerate her Oriental qualities somewhat, and the color is washed out. She's a rather small girl--her sons will loom over her--quick, athletic, practical, stoical, and, I strongly suspect, capable of walking behind a Kankali all day, skinning a sheep and broiling it while making a coat out of the hide, and, just before rolling over for a few hours' sound sleep, casually informing her man that she presented him with another child during the Noon halt. In short, I love her and you will please have the goodness to admire her appropriately.

It was a great pleasure meeting your wife, sir, and renewing the brief acquaintance you and I established during those thirty seconds or so that we met in Campbell's office--apparently just long enough for him to tell you I was a Polish aviator. There was a time when I would have loved being an aviator, but as a good Lithuanian I can hardly say I am flattered about the rest of it.

Incidentally, now that I'm an editor, why don't I make a noise like one? If you would care to enlarge on your Seattle speech, turning it into a book that could be called *"Red America--Your Life If The Commies Win,"* or, alternatively, *"The Soviet Pattern For America"*--I imagine the actual title would be less sensational than the former, less ambiguous than the latter--we would be delighted to pay you for it and publish it.

<p class="section-break">✲ ✲ ✲</p>

Our top rate--which is what we would pay you, of course, is a $1500.00 advance against royalties on the first 100,000 copies, with half of that, and a contract, going to you on our receipt of a satisfactory brief outline and portion. The other half on receipt of the completed book. Royalties on copies after the first 100,000 would have to be arranged between your agent and our publisher. It's a very fast check, if that makes any difference, and I reach quick decisions.

We put out what I think is the best newsstand softcover package in the business--I know you've seen at least one, but I'm sending you some more under separate cover--and what I would like to do is put out a colorful but dignified book, with your photo and credentials as a traveler in Soviet Russia, etc., running down the left half of the cover--somewhat in the manner of the B. Traven short story book I'm sending you.

What I think I would like you to do is make assertions about life in an occupied America, backed up by anecdotes of actual conditions observed on your Russian trip and by references to known historical facts of recent years. --I say, this is what I think. But I expect you have better ideas along this line than I do. This kind of book--this kind of Regency Book, anyhow--needs to crackle with the author's absolute conviction that what he's saying is the absolute and important truth. Accordingly, I would not want to stand in the way of your organizing this book any way you felt was best.

I hope I've succeeded in interesting you.

But whether I have or not, any time you're in the area and would like to be royally entertained at Regency expense, do not hesitate to pick up a 'phone and call the number at the head of this letter. I would give you a home address, except that I'm still living out of the back seat of my car and sleeping on a friend's foldout couch. That will have been taken care of by the end of the month.

Incidentally, if you should be in the New York area November 18, there is a farewell party for me at the home of Fred Pohl, 386 West Front Street, Red Bank, N.J. Shadyside 1-5917. Attainable by train from Penn Station in New York, or bus from the 34th Street Greyhound Terminal. I'll be speaking at the Philadelphia SFS conference that afternoon, but I wouldn't miss one of Carol Pohl's parties no matter who it was for.

my very best to you and Ginny,

{{< figure src="signature_aj.png" alt="AJ" caption="AJ" >}}

A.J. Budrys, impersonating an Editor-In-Chief

**Notes**

**Harlan Ellison at Regency** — Before AJ, Harlan Ellison briefly held the editor's post at Regency Books in 1961. His tenure was, by all accounts, creative but administratively chaotic — as AJ delicately hints.

**Regency Books** — A paperback imprint run by William L. Hamling, who also published the SF magazines *Imagination* and *Imaginative Tales*. Regency occupied an odd niche: it published provocative adult fiction and occasional science fiction, with production values Budrys genuinely believed were the best on the newsstand. RAH later asks whether Regency is "a Hamling enterprise" — confirming he already suspected it.

**"Red America" proposal** — AJ's suggested title *"Red America — Your Life If The Commies Win"* reflects the aggressive Cold War paperback market; titles like this sold briskly in the early 1960s. RAH ultimately declined, recommending Kornbluth's *Not This August* instead.

**Fred Pohl's home in Red Bank** — 386 West Front Street, Red Bank, New Jersey. Fred and his third wife Carol hosted well-known SF community gatherings here.

**Kankali** — An evocative reference to nomadic Central Asian peoples; AJ uses it to paint a portrait of his wife Eddie's practical toughness. The Kankali (Qangly) were a medieval Turkic confederation associated with the Kazakh steppe.

**"impersonating an Editor-In-Chief"** — AJ's characteristic self-deprecating closing; he was genuinely still finding his footing in a demanding new role.
