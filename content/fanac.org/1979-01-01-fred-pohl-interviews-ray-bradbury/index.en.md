**Fred Pohl:** You did a couple of other early science fiction films.

**Ray Bradbury:** I did *The Beast from 20,000 Fathoms* and *It Came from Outer Space*. That was my first job at Universal. No, they had an idea, which I didn't think much of. And I had a couple of meetings with them. And I went to work at some ridiculously low rate, maybe $200 a week, which is really terrible. And at a certain point, we came to a sort of impasse on the idea.

And I said, "I'll tell you what. I'll write two versions of the story for you. I'll write one to please you — and I think it's not a good idea — and then I want to please myself. And then in two weeks from now, I'll turn in both to you. And then you have to decide which way we go. And if you choose your way, I'll leave. And if you choose my way, I'll go and do the whole — not a script, but treatment."

Which was more than a script, because I gave them a hundred pages, like a fool. Well, at the end of two weeks, I turned them in. And they were smart enough to see the difference. And they said, "Go your way." I said, "Good." So I did the treatment. And they were afraid, though — the treatment was fine — they were afraid to let me do the final screenplay. So they brought Harry Essex in. And as he admitted to me later, it was a dream for him, because he adapted a hell of a lot of it directly out of the treatment. I'd given them more than enough material. And at least he — it was charming of him to say that I was a good influence on the final screenplay.

**Fred Pohl:** Did he follow your treatment pretty much?

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah, pretty much.

**Fred Pohl:** Is that the first one you did?

**Ray Bradbury:** Very first. And I was careful to keep the monsters in the background. And like a fool, I warned them not to bring the monsters out in the light. As soon as you do that, you're not scared anymore. They're really good films. Like *The Thing* is scary up to a point, because you can't see this thing that's looking. Or maybe you just see a hand through the door. As soon as you bring the thing out in the light, it's funny.

And the great thing about a film like *The Haunting*, Robert Wise, about 15 years ago, is one of the greatest horror movies ever made, because you never see a damn thing during the whole film. All you hear are wonderful sounds. You see doors creak and knobs turn and the wind blowing, and that's it. So everything's in direction. And of course, Val Lewton taught us about that in all of his films he produced, like *Isle of the Dead*, *Cat People*, *Curse of the Cat People*, *I Walk with a Zombie*, *The Leopard Man*. Those were all indirectly done. They used light and shadow.

**Fred Pohl:** That's why I think radio is better than TV or sometimes science fiction.

**Ray Bradbury:** Well, that's where Robert Wise was so smart in *The Haunting*. That's photographic radio.

**Fred Pohl:** You called the people or the beings in *It Came from Outer Space* "monsters." Do you really think they are monsters? It seems that that was one film of that time that really worked for you.

**Ray Bradbury:** No, I use that word very freely, because the people in the town thought they were monsters. And of course, *It Came from Outer Space* was the genesis for *Close Encounters*.

**Fred Pohl:** You'll admit that?

**Ray Bradbury:** Oh, yeah, he's wonderful. He's such a dear young man. When I saw *Close Encounters* a year ago, I was so enthused. I was just mad for the film.

**Fred Pohl:** And you said it was a religious experience.

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah, and I was in the studio...

**Fred Pohl:**  I think we saw it just the day or two after.

**Ray Bradbury:**  Yeah. And I called *The Times* and I said, "I've got to review this." They said, "We already have a review." I said, "If you don't let me review it, I'm going to come down and blow the place up." And they said, "OK, OK."

So I called Spielberg the next morning and said, "Hey, I've just written a review of your film. And I've got to come see you and hug you and kiss you and tell you what a genius you are." He said, "Come on out." So I went to the studio, and when I walked in his office, the first thing he said was, "How did you like your film?" I said, "What?" He says, "Sure, of course, it's your film. There's so much of you through all this, including *It Came from Outer Space*."

And I said, "Jeez, that's so wonderful of you to say. And it's good to know. That's really what we want in this life, isn't it? Not the financial rewards. They're nice, but they don't last. What you really want is to feel that part of you is going to be around through the next generation. And when a kid like this, who's that brilliant, is open enough and generous enough to say a thing like that — boy, oh boy, I'm a very rich man."

And so anyway, going back to *It Came from Outer Space*: the fools created monsters and showed them to you. And every time they do that, you back off and you're not afraid. The better stuff is little tiny touches that I wrote in the treatment of them going up and listening to the telephone wires and the wind rustling. And you get a sense of the desert. I'm very pleased about those sections. And I'm pleased about the message at the end — letting them get away and saying, "Maybe someday they'll come back and there will be this meeting when they can understand one another."

**Fred Pohl:** So in its time.

**Ray Bradbury:** So then, of course, that same year, a fluke — *Beast from 20,000 Fathoms* — got made. And what happened was Ray Harryhausen, I heard, was working on this project. And his producer called me. They had a script which they had finished. And they called me in, and the producer was very quick-talking and preoccupied and said, "We've got the script. Are you interested?" I said, "I don't know. I haven't read it." He said, "Well, we'll take an hour and go in the next room and read it and tell us if you'll revise it." And some sort of dinosaur film.

So because Ray Harryhausen was connected with it, I said, "I'm sure, what the hell." So I went in the next office and spent an hour reading this rather dreadful script. And I came out, and he said, "Well, can you help us? Will you revise it?" I said, "Maybe. I don't know." I said, "Incidentally, this story is very much like this short story of mine that was in *The Saturday Evening Post* a year ago called *The Beast from 100,000 Fathoms*."

And a funny look came over his face, and I realized that inadvertently — I would gather that somehow or other, my idea got involved in their script. I don't know the genesis of this whole thing. I don't believe it was a direct act of anyone's taking a thing. I think it was inadvertent.

**Fred Pohl:** And it's done, and it doesn't happen more often.

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah. And so without my even raising my voice or saying anything more, the next day they sent me a telegram and bought the story. So that's how the film got made. I had no more to do with it from that point out.

**Fred Pohl:** You didn't have any part of it?

**Ray Bradbury:** No, and my story wound up being about 45 seconds on the screen. If they'd been wise, they'd have used more of it, because it would have been terrifically visual and it would have added some aesthetic touch to the center of the film. So that's how that film got made. Of course, the best thing about it is Ray Harryhausen's special effects. Ray is always the best part of the films that he makes.

**Fred Pohl:** How about before you did *Moby Dick* or anything else?

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah, and it's interesting

**Fred Pohl:**  that they still paid attention to you. I thought they didn't care about anybody who hadn't done the film.

**Fred Pohl:** Well, it's all very very fluky.

One of the big influences in my life was Norman Corwin, the radio producer, writer, director — the greatest. Much greater than Orson Welles, of course. The whole history of radio is tied into Norman Corwin.

So when I had my first book published when I was 27 — *Dark Carnival* — I looked up Corwin, got his address and phone number, sent him a copy of the book, and wrote him a note saying, "If you like my book as much as I love your radio work, I'd like to buy you a drink some afternoon." A week later, he called me and said, "You're not buying me drinks, I'm buying you supper." And we've been fast friends ever since.

**Fred Pohl:** I didn't know he was still alive.

**Ray Bradbury:** Oh, yeah, he's vital. He's 68.

**Fred Pohl:** Is that old?

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah.

**Ray Bradbury:** He was in his twenties when he started on radio. And he was only — let's see — 1946. I was 26.

**Fred Pohl:** Was *Lights Out* his, or was that...

**Ray Bradbury:** That's Arch Oboler.

**Fred Pohl:** What was his [Corwin's] job? He had a series.

**Ray Bradbury:** Corwin had a whole bunch of things on him called *CBS Presents Corwin*, *More by Corwin*, *13 by Corwin* — they're all in book form. And then he did, of course, the celebration for Victory in Europe.

**Fred Pohl:** \[..\]

**Ray Bradbury:**  Yeah. And then he did *My Client Curly*, the Cary Grant thing about the little caterpillar. And Fred Allen starred in it on radio for Corwin, and then Cary Grant took over and played in the film.

Anyway, Corwin was a huge influence on me. And I did a lot of radio writing for *Suspense*, which was one of the best of its type on radio in those days. And they had people like Orson Welles on there. And I worked with Ida Lupino and Agnes Moorehead one time or another.

So I went to a United Nations broadcast by Corwin in 1949. And right behind me, in walked John Huston and his new wife. And they sat there. And Jesus, I went all out of my mind because I'd seen *The Maltese Falcon* 12 times and *The Treasure of the Sierra Madre* 20 times. And he was my superhero.

And Norman offered to introduce me. And I refused. Because I said, "You know, I'll gush over him. And I have no way to prove my talent right now. I have only one book. And when I publish two more books, then you can introduce me."

So two more years went by. And I published *The Martian Chronicles* and *The Illustrated Man*. And when I published *The Illustrated Man*, I called my agent and I said, "Now I want to meet Huston." So they set up a meeting for me at Mike Romanoff's on St. Valentine's night, 1951.

And Huston had just finished *The Red Badge of Courage* and was going off to Africa the next week to do *The African Queen* with Katie Hepburn. So I had dinner with him. And I said, "You know, I love you. I love what you do. I want to work for you someday. Here are my books. Here's my proof of my love. Take them with you and write me back if you like them."

And he wrote me back from London and then from Africa. And he said, "Yep, it's good stuff. And someday we'll do something together." And we corresponded twice in the next two years. And he came back to town in August of 1953, called me up to the hotel, put a drink in my hand. He said, "What are you doing right now?" I said, "Nothing. I just finished a new book — *Fahrenheit 451*." He says, "You got some extra time the next six months?" I said, "Yes." He says, "Well, kid, how would you like to write *Moby Dick*?"
 
And I said, "I don't know. I've never been able to read the damn thing." So I said — I was flabbergasted. And strangely enough, two months before, I was wandering around the house one night, picked up a copy of *Moby Dick* in our library, and leafed through and said to my wife, "I wonder when I'll get around to reading this thing." Well, about eight weeks later. And then we'd seen John Huston in *Moby Dick* at the Silent Movie Theater three weeks earlier. I hadn't seen it since I was 10 years old. That silent version came out in 1930.

So I went home, got out the copy of *Moby Dick*, and read about half of it, stayed up until 2 or 3 in the morning, and discovered, of course, the miraculous thing. There's so much of the Bible there — and having been raised with the Bible — and so much of Shakespeare there — and having been raised with Shakespeare — that my love grew as I wandered through the pages. I didn't start at the beginning. I started in the middle and started wandering all over the place and found beautiful stuff about the spirit spout sprinkling the midnight deeps, and the whole section on the whiteness of the whale and the polar Antarctic and Arctic whitenesses, and the whitenesses of panics and terrors, and the dreams of strange fish that rise blind because they have no need of eyes from the subterranean seas.

And then I turned back to the section where Ahab is standing at the rail, and he says, "It's a mild, mild day and a mild-looking sky, and the wind smells as if it blew from the shadow of the Andes, where the mowers have lain down with their scythes." And I turned back to the beginning — "Call me Ishmael" — and I was in love.

So the next day I was able to go to him and said, "You know, it's huge, but I think I'm old enough now. I'm 33. Yes, I'd like to do it."

So three weeks later, we left for France and for Ireland and spent the whole winter there. And the long way around it, the point was that I said to John, "Well, how come you selected me? How much of it was a fluke?" And he said, "Well, you know that story of yours, *The Fog Horn*, about the sea monster that comes out of the sea? He said, 'That's pure Melville.' And when I saw that in *The Golden Apples of the Sun* — I'd sent him that book later on — and a couple of other of my dinosaur stories had the quality, a certain quality of Melville. So he based it on that.

And then I think the extra little factor that made him pick me is that he wanted to irritate his friends. He'd just be able to go around and say, 'I've hired a young science fiction writer, and he's going to do *Moby Dick* — and watch their faces.' So there's that side of John which loves to taunt people. And so it was kind of wonderful.

It was very difficult because I had to read the book nine times in seven months. And parts of it I read 40 times, certain scenes. So they got into my blood system. And then at the end of seven months of reading and writing, I got out of bed one morning in London, walked over, looked in the mirror, and said, "I am Herman Melville." And on that day, I wrote the last third of the script in one day. Everything fell into place. All the metaphors coalesced — my metaphors, his metaphors. And I ran across London in a frenzy of excitement. And I threw the script in John's lap. I said, "Read that." And he read it. "That's it. We're not going to change anything."

**Fred Pohl:** Real unusual for you to write somebody else's story.

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah. I've done other work. I've done one script I'm very proud of, which has never been made. I did *The Dreamers* by Roger Manvell. Did you ever happen?... Well, it's a voodoo story. It came out about 21 years ago. And I've done a lot of screenplays that haven't been made yet, and they're damn good. I'm a pretty good judge. I know when I've done something.

**Fred Pohl:** Isn't that frustrating, though, when you try to write...

**Ray Bradbury:** That's why you try to stay away from screenwriting. It's much better.

**Fred Pohl:** It drives you crazy.

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah. I've done a lot of good work. And it'll all get made eventually. But some of it's a little late in time now.

But I did *The Rock Cried Out* for Sir Carol Reed, whose work I was mad for — also *The Third Man* and *Odd Man Out* — a lot of lovely films like that. And so I spent a whole summer in London working with Carol Reed on *The Rock Cried Out*. And it was way ahead of its time. It came out of the McCarthy period, of course. I wrote it back in '52. And had a rough time selling it to anyone because everyone was scared stiff in the magazines. *Mademoiselle* kept it for four or five months and didn't have enough guts to publish it.

**Fred Pohl:** And you are worry about it?.

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah. And they finally sent it back to me. I was so enraged with them for holding it so long. I knew they were cowards because McCarthy was still riding high. It wasn't until '54 that the Army got in. And so I wrote to *Mademoiselle* and demanded they pay me for holding the story. And they did. They were such cowards — they sent me a check for $400, but they didn't say what it was for. It said "for travel expenses." They still were so chicken. They didn't want to name the story.

So anyway, Carol Reed read it. Graham Greene gave a copy of the story to Carol Reed. Carol Reed called Hollywood — talked to Harold Hecht — asking about me. And Harold says, "What a coincidence — Carol, Ray is sitting in the office right now." Because I was in there to do some consulting work on *White Hunter, Black Heart*, a Peter Viertel book on Huston.

So Carol flew over. And we spent a week together and got on beautifully. I went back to London, did a screenplay in 10 weeks, which everyone loved. But the studios wouldn't finance it. Because it was the story of the white man and his wife in a South American small town in a dark culture, where when the atom war breaks out, the shoe's on the other foot — they've got to take over and wait on tables and do what the black man did, or the colored man.

**Fred Pohl:** So the script is still around?

**Ray Bradbury:** Still around. And then in order to get the rights back on it, I did a script free for them on *The Dreamers*. But first I had to rewrite the novel. Because novels quite often work in their own terms. But on the screen, you've got to rise to a peak and then drop off. And his novel was written much too flat, and he gave away too many details too soon, which spoiled the fun. So I had to figure out ways of saving information from the front and putting it in the middle, things in the middle at the very end, so the thing would rise to a peak. Then I added some things to it which were fascinating.

And I think it's a little late in time for that, because a lot of the stuff I put in that screenplay were used in *The Exorcist* three years, four years ago. So if we did our film now, even though mine was written 21 years ago, we've seen it already. Certain effects with voices coming out of throats that we've seen on the screen now. So I've got four or five screenplays that haven't been made.

**Fred Pohl:** Adapted from other people?

**Ray Bradbury:** No, mostly from my own.

**Fred Pohl:** You should publish on screenplays.

**Ray Bradbury:** Well, I suppose I could eventually. This time that has come where people are curious about screenplays.

**Fred Pohl:** I think Nabokov finally published his screenplay for *Lolita* after agonizing over the fact that it wasn't used.

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah, it would be good to.

I had an experience eight or nine years ago. It was a comedy of errors. I bumped into an animator in a drug store. And he said, "What are you doing?" I said, "Oh, working on a play." And I said, "What are you doing?" He said, "Well, I'm going to make a film with Picasso."

I said, "Oh, boy, that sounds wonderful. Who are you affiliated with?" Well, he was affiliated with Bill Cosby and his company — Campbell, Silver, Cosby. And they had just made millions with Cosby's records and personal appearances and TV. And they had money to throw around like drunken sailors. So I said, "Gee, how far along are you on this?" He said, "Oh, we're just getting started. We're going to do some animation."

I said, "Well, I've got a short story about Picasso. It'd be perfect. And why don't I send it to you?" So he says, "Fine." So I sent them the short story. And they called me a couple of days later. "God, this is just what we need for the film."

So I said, "Fine, if you want me to write the screenplay — *The Search for Picasso* — a guy who's madly in love with Picasso and goes over to Europe with his wife to try and meet him. And they are always missing each other until the very end. And they come upon Picasso drawing in the sand on the beach, and they have their own original there. And then the tide comes in and wipes out the Picasso." It's a very fragile storyline.

And my original thing with them, I said, "Look, keep it to half an hour, just a little short film. And don't try and do anything too long." Well, there's no way of financing that. And they said, "We'll go to TV. Maybe we can do an hour film for TV. And how about that?" I said, "OK, it might just stretch to an hour, fine, but nothing more than that."

Well, they couldn't do anything in TV. And then they went to Albert Finney. And Finney said, "Hell, I'll do it if I get to meet Picasso." And on the strength of Finney's name, they got their own money together and studio money — a million, million and a half. And we started production. I started writing the script. And they flew over Serge Bourguignon, a young French director. And he had done *Sundays and Cybèle*. Got an Academy Award for it.

And I got on fine with everyone. And everyone loved the script. They said, "My God, it's a work of genius." All these lovely terms they used. And they went to France. And they shot the film. And I trusted them. And they came back three or four months later. And they ran a rough cut of it for me. And there was just nothing left. They had torn up my script every day.

I asked Bourguignon, "What happened to scene number 12 that leads into scene number 13?" "Bene, très bien." I said, "OK, fine. Where's scene number 17, scene number 21, scene number 25?" "Bene, très bien." So I said, "Serge, you're bene"...

**Fred Pohl:** Was that Picasso's something?

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah. It was such a bad film. It was never released.

**Fred Pohl:** I've seen it on television.

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah, but it was never in theaters. It was so bad. And I went to Warner Brothers after they'd put it on the shelf for a couple of years and offered to work free for two months with an editor to try to save it. Because with a good cutting job, there's still enough of a nice film there. It would never be great, but it could be good. It would be fun. I had a lovely score by Michel Legrand, which is still around — "Summer Me, Winter Me" is one of the nicest songs that came out of it, sung by Barbra Streisand. I hear that on the radio all the time. But that's all that's left of the film.

But Warners wouldn't let me touch it. And they finally sold it to CBS, and it's been on twice. But again, even when you do the script, and everyone says they love it, there's no guarantee that the film is going to get done correctly.

**Fred Pohl:** Yes. Did you have anything to do with *Fahrenheit 451*?

**Ray Bradbury:** No, I just — since I had worked on the novel twice — the short version, *The Fireman*, and the long version. And then I did the play with Lawton, which didn't work. And when Truffaut came along, we originally were going to do *The Illustrated Man*. I wish we had. But Truffaut couldn't get financing for — we'll get to *The Illustrated Man* in a moment.

But I had meetings with Truffaut in New York back around 1963 or 1964, I believe it was. I came back there. We got on just fine. And I was originally supposed to go to France and spend part of the summer there writing *The Illustrated Man* for the screen. And my wife and I were very excited about the prospect, but it never came about.

So then about a year later, Truffaut came back and said, "Look, what about *Fahrenheit*? I think I can get financing for that." And I said, "Fine." And he said, "Do you want to do the screenplay?" I said, "No. I've worked on it too long and I'm too close to it. I wouldn't do a good job for you. I'll trust you. I've seen your films. Do it with your collaborator."

And so they then proceeded to do a script. And I heard all kinds of rumors about casting and financing. And I found out later — combinations of the truth. I mean, everyone has their version of it. It's *Rashomon* time, isn't it? Truffaut complaining about the difficulty of this actor and that, and they complain about Truffaut. But I gather what happened is that Truffaut went to... Werner...

**Fred Pohl:** Oskar Werner.

**Ray Bradbury:** Oskar Werner. And offered him the script. And Oskar Werner said, "Well, no. You haven't stuck close enough to the novel. I don't want to do that."

So rather than change the script, Truffaut went to Belmondo, I believe, but Belmondo was busy. And I think he approached some other actor, and he was busy. And he came back to Oskar Werner and said, "If you take the role, we'll revise it back closer to the novel." I don't know if that's true or not. I think it might be. At least Oskar Werner told me that. And I'm thankful to him if that's true.

So they proceeded, and you see the final product, which is flawed, but beautiful. It's a haunting picture. And when you think of the theme of it, it's a very unusual film to have been made. Because the theme of the film is a man who fell in love with books — not with women.

**Fred Pohl:** I don't think any American would have made it.

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah.

**Fred Pohl:** Certainly not like that.

**Ray Bradbury:** No, very unusual. And the last scenes are some of the most beautiful scenes ever put on the screen.

**Fred Pohl:** You want to flip your papers over here?

**Ray Bradbury:** I sent him my script, and I said, you know, nothing's happening. And he says, "Well, when the option comes up, I'll buy it. I love the script, and I want to direct it."

So — excuse me \[a door ring on the background\]

 **Ray Bradbury:** Spielberg was already the optionist. And all of a sudden, bang, a gigantic check came in and Kirk Douglas and his son picked up the final option, which scared the hell out of me for a moment. I thought, my God, what's going to happen next? I ran to them — and they had the right to do this, it was in the contract — but they had indicated maybe they're going to let it lapse. And I ran to them immediately and I said, "Hey, I want Spielberg." And they go, "Of course." And we went on through.

So I went out to see Spielberg the next day, and we still have a contract with him. But he says he will do it — it's a matter of scheduling now. And I hope maybe late this year we'll get it done.

**Ray Bradbury:** That's where I was going to do it.

**Fred Pohl:** You did an awful lot of script writing.

**Ray Bradbury:** Yeah.

**Fred Pohl:** And you did a lot of writing — did any of that show up?

**Ray Bradbury:** No, there wasn't. See, again, that's one of those cases where — see, I worked at MGM 18 years ago on one version of it. Everyone's owned it. *The Martian Magazines* was one time — Kirk Douglas owned it. It was going to be a TV series 21 years ago. And thank God nothing ever came of that. And then David Susskind owned it for six months. I was going to write it into a musical for him. Yeah, with Sidney Carroll helping out. And thank God that didn't work out, because Susskind wanted comedy — lots of yachts, man. And I finally gave him his money back and told him to get lost.

And then MGM owned it for about six months, and we worked on a script of it for them, which wasn't right. Because I tried to tie it together too much and I tried to carry over characters. But it didn't work — I was forcing it. But the good thing about that — they also hired me during that same period to write the narration for *King of Kings*, the new version. They had no ending for the film. And I said, "Well, have you read the Bible?" \[?\]
