**Context:** Recorded most likely at the 1968 Worldcon (Baycon, Oakland, CA, 1–5 August 1968), shortly before the publication of *Stand on Zanzibar* (Doubleday, 7 September 1968). Brunner covers his early career — first sales, RAF service, editorial work in London — and the 1959 nuclear weapons exhibition that shaped his worldview. He discusses his literary influences (H.G. Wells, Kipling) and comments at length on contemporary writers: Philip K. Dick, Fritz Leiber, James Blish, Clifford Simak, Samuel R. Delany, J.G. Ballard, and Roger Zelazny.

## Interview

**Frederik Pohl:** How long have you been writing science fiction?

**John Brunner:** Well, it depends what you count from — the first story I started, or the first story I sold.

I discovered science fiction when I was six, and somebody left a copy of *The War of the Worlds* in my nursery. And I was about age nine, I suppose, so I decided I wasn't getting enough stuff and started a science fiction story of my own, which I never finished. I was 13 when I got my first rejection slip, and 17 when I sold my first novel.

**Frederik Pohl:** Will you submit this first story again?

**John Brunner:** The first story that I got a rejection slip on was from — what was then — *Startling*?

**Frederik Pohl:** What was your first novel that you sold at 17?

**John Brunner:** I'm glad to say it appeared under a pseudonym and a title I didn't wish on it, and it is sunk without trace.

**Frederik Pohl:** I did that too — I wrote for ten years under pen names, and consequently I can deny them all.

**Frederik Pohl:** So we're up to about 17, which is when — roughly 1951?

**John Brunner:** In 1951.

**Frederik Pohl:** And you were still in school at the time?

**John Brunner:** I was, yes. I left school at the end of 1951.

**Frederik Pohl:** What happened after you left school?

**John Brunner:** From then until being dropped into the Air Force, I tried to mortar up some of the gaps in my education. The chief reason I quit school was because I felt I simply was not being educated. And I'm certain I've taught myself a great deal more since I quit school than was ever forced into my brain when I was there.

Then I had two years in the Air Force, where, as a conscript, a miserable place called Bletchley, in Buckinghamshire, halfway between Oxford and Cambridge — but otherwise going nowhere.

I did very little writing when I was in the Air Force, but during the few months preceding my service I had made my first sales to American magazines — starting with "Thou Good and Faithful," which appeared under the name John Loxmith in *Astounding Science Fiction* from March 1953, and then, I think, "The Wanton of Argus" in the old *Two Complete Science-Adventure Books*.

And I went on trying to write while I was in the Air Force, but I found it very difficult. When I came out of the Air Force, I had a little more success in selling. During the following year, I moved to London under the mistaken impression that I was already a writer. I was living in a two-pounds-a-week room with an income of about £4 a week, and I learned an awful lot of ways of cooking potatoes.

I was just about to give up and go home when the phone rang one morning, and it was John Christopher — who was then working for the Industrial Diamond Information Bureau. He wanted the telephone number of a friend of mine. I said, "Why do you want it?" And he said, "Well, I want to give him a job." And I said, "Will I do instead?" And he said, "You'll do fine. Come along tomorrow." So I immediately doubled my income, and I worked there for about six months, getting on very well with John Christopher — his real name, of course, is Sam Youd — a very nice guy.

Then I went to the Books for Pleasure group, under Jonathan Burke, who's also written quite a lot of \[science fiction\] books.

**Frederik Pohl:** Books for Pleasure?

**John Brunner:** Books for Pleasure, yes. They're a publishing and distribution house. It's now absorbed into the Daily Mirror empire as a book subsidiary, but then it was mainly a cheap-imprint house. It was printing, oddly enough, in Czechoslovakia. As I say, I was there for about two years, doing general editorial work.

And in November 1958 — actually, when I was in hospital recovering from an appendectomy — I sent a letter of resignation, because I had just made my first novel sale here in the States, and I felt I could reasonably risk trying my luck as a freelance again.

**Frederik Pohl:** This was what year?

**John Brunner:** This was 1958, the end of 1958. And in the summer of that year I married Marjorie, whom I'd met about 15 months previously. And she continued to work for a while after we got married, but I'd have to say it was only about two years or so before my income became steady enough for her to come and work for me instead.

**Frederik Pohl:** So you worked for about 1960?

**John Brunner:** Yes. And the previous year — in 1969 — I had an experience which has left a lasting impression on me, and I think a lasting influence on my writing.

A group of architects and designers in Hampstead, which is the borough of London where I live, prepared an exhibition about nuclear weapons and the effects of possible nuclear war, using many photographs from Japanese sources which had never been published in Europe. And we decided that we would prepare interchangeable four-language captions for this, and we took it around seven countries of Europe — Sweden, Denmark, West Germany, Switzerland, France, Holland, and Belgium. The whole trip took about four months. The preparation of the exhibition in its multilingual version also took about four months, which meant that for two-thirds of the year I was living, night and day, with images of horror and destruction.

And perhaps as a direct result of this, I think in my stories my future worlds tend to be unified and pacified — not so much from wishful thinking, but because, writing mainly about the future, I have a lasting interest in there *being* a future for me to write about.

**Frederik Pohl:** That brings us up to the end of 1959, 1960.

**John Brunner:** Well, I settled down to what's been my more or less standard working pattern ever since. I write a majority of science fiction, but I also write a great many other things. I've had a straight novel published in Britain, and I'm negotiating for another one over here at the moment. I've published thrillers, espionage stories, articles, reviews — and now I write and give public readings of quite a lot of poetry.

**Frederik Pohl:** Oh, really?

**John Brunner:** Recently I've done readings at the Hornsey Arts Festival, the Brighton Arts Festival, two poets' picnics — *"Z-E-Y-S-E-Y"* — yes, and quite a large festival, two poets' picnics; and in October I'm doing a reading for the Poetry Society for the first time.

**Frederik Pohl:** If you were not a writer, what would you like to be?

**John Brunner:** This is a question which has sometimes been put to me, and I've never come up with a really satisfactory answer, because I've wanted to be a writer and been fairly sure that I was going to be one since I was about nine years old, as I mentioned.

My feeling is that probably, if I hadn't managed to get into writing as a steady job, so to speak, I would probably have tried to go and work for the BBC — first in radio and later in television. I think this would have been perhaps in its way an equally satisfying career.

**Frederik Pohl:** Not a political career? You're a very political person.

**John Brunner:** I don't really regard myself as a political person in the party-political sense. Perhaps the French word *engagé* is the best description of my attitude. I feel socially involved, without being committed to any particular political party or political dogma.

**Frederik Pohl:** Okay. Is there anything you would like to say — preach a sermon, or an expression of opinion, or anything else?

**John Brunner:** Not particularly. I think in fact I've covered the reason why my future worlds tend to be rather specifically...

**Frederik Pohl:** About science fiction then.. Are there any science fiction writers who you think are — who are the big men to you in science fiction? Who are the ones you've enjoyed as you began writing, and who are the ones you think made an influential ?

**John Brunner:** It's difficult to assess the actual relative degree of impact of the various science fiction writers I read when I first decided I was going to try my hand at it. Obviously Wells, because I discovered him so young — same as everybody else. But perhaps one of the most significant science fiction writers I read when I was a child was, of all people, Kipling. It's not often realized that something like one story in seven that Kipling published was science fiction or fantasy.

And Kipling is perhaps the most completely equipped writer ever to tackle the short story in the English language. Some of his later work was so oblique and so full of implications that it's hard to believe it was by the same man who had earlier written *Plain Tales from the Hills*.

**Frederik Pohl:** What would you go to name some of the science fiction stories?

**John Brunner:** Well, of course, his best-known are things like *With the Night Mail* and *As Easy as A.B.C.*, which are set in the 21st century. But additionally I would class as science fiction stories like ".007," in which the protagonist is a locomotive; *The Ship That Found Herself*, in which there are no human characters whatsoever — just the constituent parts of a ship on her shakedown voyage, which come together at the end as the ship speaks with one voice; *Unprofessional*, from a very late collection of his called *Limits and Renewals*, in which a group of doctors discovers that there really is some influence exerted by the moon on the course of disease. And so on — a remarkably wide range of what would be regarded as science fiction themes, had they been written by a science fiction writer.

**Frederik Pohl:** Do any of the new writers impress you particularly?

**John Brunner:** Of the current writers — before I get onto the new writers — Philip K. Dick is so good a novelist that I don't think I recall any of his books which didn't give me enormous pleasure. Some of them, of course, like *The Man in the High Castle* stand out above the rest of his output. But I regard him as an extraordinarily stimulating writer with a really pyrotechnical imagination.

I also enjoy Fritz Leiber; James Blish at his best; Clifford Simak. Oh, many, many writers have given me a great deal of enjoyment.

Among the newer writers, I think one of the smartest things I ever did in my life was to send a fan letter to Chip Delany, because it resulted in him calling on us on his way back from Greece, and we became very close friends. I enjoyed his writing enormously. I feel that he needs perhaps a little more exercise and discipline in his craft, but his imagination the richness of his vocabulary struck me the first time I read one of his books.

I also enjoy the work of J. G. Ballard on our side of the water. But of course it's largely a coincidence that Jimmy Ballard's work has been published so widely in science fiction magazines. He doesn't really regard himself as a science fiction writer. He regards himself more as a commentator on the contemporary scene. And I suspect that it was largely fortuitous that science fiction magazines offered an outlet for his early stories. He certainly seems to have made a lasting impression on the field — I don't know of any other writer whose name can be relied on to generate so much argument so rapidly when it crops up.

**Frederik Pohl:** What about Zelazny? — no, you don't have to comment if you have something else you want to say about it.

**John Brunner:** I enjoy Roger Zelazny's work very much, but I find it rather uneven. He has a great gift for vivid description, but occasionally this slides over into purple prose, and at that point I lose him.

He, like Chip, is somebody of whom I have tremendous hopes. But it's been my experience that we ought to get back to the old idea of a *masterpiece* — that is to say, a task one sets oneself in order to demonstrate that one has fully mastered the basics of the writing craft.

Picasso, to my mind, could never have become the seminal influence that he has on modern art had he not been one of the great portrait painters of his generation.

And I have a feeling that I have been fortunate in coming up the slow way through science fiction — that is to say, none of my early books achieved immediate wide acclaim. They just sold, and they brought me a steady living. And now, finally, after ten years of freelancing, I think I've done a book which will stand up head and shoulders above anything else I've ever written. This is *Stand on Zanzibar*, from Doubleday. I think that this is a book that I could not possibly have written — could not even have attempted — until I'd published at least several previous novels.

**Frederik Pohl:** *Stand on Zanzibar?* Well, if you are —

**John Brunner:** 7th of September, I believe, is the publication date.

**Frederik Pohl:** Well, excuse me.
