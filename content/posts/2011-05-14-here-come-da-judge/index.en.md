## Here Come da Judge


{{< figure src="mark-ciavarella.jpg" alt="Mark Ciavarella" caption="Mark Ciavarella" >}}


Now let’s talk a little bit about the justice system.  Start with the traditional opening call:  “All rise!  His Honor’s coming in!”  That’s Pennsylvania judge Mark Ciavarella we’re talking about, and unless his attorneys succeed in an unlikely appeal, what he’s coming into will be jail.


As a juvenile court judge, Mark Ciavarella had a great reputation for dispensing strict discipline — as, for instance, a ten-year-old girl found when she accidentally set fire to her bedroom, and got a month in the slammer for it, while a 13-year-old boy got 48 days for throwing a piece of meat at his mother’s boyfriend.  Et many cetera.  The judge’s practices were apparently viewed by him as a form of tough love, hurting kids to help them, so his boosters said — until he went on trial for racketeering and got convicted.  Then it came out that the judge was taking  kickbacks from the juvie detention operators whose cells he was  keeping profitably occupied.


Boy, I’ll bet those kids have learned their lesson from the judge’s hard-edged, if profitable, sentencing practices.  The only thing is I’m not quite sure what lesson it was.


But it’s all straightened out now.  The State Supreme Court, which apparently had been napping during all those years while local papers ran stories about Ciavarella’s demonstrable bias, finally woke up long enough to take action.


What action did they take?  You’d never guess.  They set aside thousands of guilty verdicts Ciavarella had issued.


That meant that many innocent people were at last freed from jail, which is certainly a good thing, but it also meant that a whole bunch of stone criminals got out, too, and that surely isn’t good at all.


See why they say justice is blind?
