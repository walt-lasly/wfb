## Where Did All the ERs Go?


{{< figure src="emergency-room.jpg" alt="If you don't have health insurance, the emergency room may be your only option for medical treatment. (Photo by Leah A. Zeldes.)" caption="If you don't have health insurance, the emergency room may be your only option for medical treatment. (Photo by Leah A. Zeldes.)" >}}


Let’s make believe you’re a poor person.  (I know that’s a stretch, but let’s go with it.)  A member of your family gets a fever and other signs of something wrong, so you want to take him to a doctor.  The trouble is the only insurance you have is government-sponsored Medicaid, and good luck trying to find a doctor who will take it.  Or maybe you don’t even have Medicaid, so you’re even worse off.  So what do you do.  Simple.  You take your sick one to the nearest emergency room, where they aren’t allowed to turn anyone down.


That’s, for you, a good solution to an otherwise hopeless problem, and over the last thirty years or so the number of people who took that step because they had no better choice in the U.S.A. went up some 35 percent. This put a big strain on the cost of running a hospital, with the result that many hospitals couldn’t afford to keep their emergency rooms open.  But they couldn’t close the ERs unless they closed the whole hospital … and now you know why 27 percent of all American hospitals (psychiatric hospitals not included) have closed their doors since 1990.


If Obamacare is allowed to proceed as the legislation directs, that problem should be pretty well solved within the next few years.  That’s assuming that the Republican Party doesn’t succeed in repealing it for the sake of higher profits for the medical industry, which they are at present trying their best to do.

- [Leah A. Zeldes](https://web.archive.org/web/20160429214502/http://www.zeldes.comm/) says:
“You take your sick one to the nearest emergency room, where they aren’t allowed to turn anyone down.”
Well, yes and no. If you show up at the ER bleeding or with broken leg, they do have to patch you up. If you show up with the kind of chronic or complex health problems that people who go a long time without health care are prone to lapse into, then they don’t have do anything about that — all they’re required to do is “stabilize” you.
Say, for example, you develop a large, impassable kidney stone — one of the most painful conditions anyone could possibly experience — and you go to the ER in screaming agony. They’ll do an expensive test to determine that’s what’s wrong with you and to make certain that the stone isn’t blocking anything that could be life threatening. They’ll give you painkillers. And then they’ll send you home, kidney stone intact.  (This is a real example.)
Nor is this free. The hospital is perfectly within its rights to ask you to pay up front and they may well try hard to get you or your accompanying loved ones to fork over some cash right in the ER if you’re uninsured. If you can’t pay, yes, they must “stabilize” you anyway, but then they can and will bill you for payment afterward. 
The hospital may have a charity program to forgive part of the bill if you submit all kinds of paperwork and qualify. But that’s only part of the bill. Nearly every doctor is an independent contractor. Everybody from the urologist to the radiologist who read your X-rays to the gastroenterologist they called in when you started vomiting from the pain will send you a separate bill.
So you wind up owing thousands of dollars in order to receive a prescription for a bottle of pills.  And everyone concerned will dun you mercilessly if you don’t pay. That’s one reason so many Americans declare bankruptcy.
   

Medicaid, you say, dave schutz? Not only is it being gutted in many states, such as Wisconsin, eligibility for Medicaid in most places requires serious poverty with no assets as well as other criteria (including, as Greg Benford notes, legal residency in the U.S.). If you are a formerly middle-class unemployed worker trying to hang on to your house, or somebody working in a low-wage job without benefits (such as a waiter or a freelance writer), you probably don’t qualify. And if you’re chronically ill, you may not be able to cope with the paperwork to try, anyway.
