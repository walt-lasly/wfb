# The Way the Future Blogs


Frederik Pohl


## The Lost Cons: #1, the Weissmuller Con


Possibly in Arizona?  Possibly late ’60s?


{{< figure src="tarzanwb.jpg" alt="Johnny Weissmuller and Maureen O'Sullivan." caption="Johnny Weissmuller and Maureen O'Sullivan." >}}


For some time now, I’ve been putting on paper recollections that I think will sooner or later go into what will be either an expanded new edition of my autobiography, [The Way the Future Was](https://web.archive.org/web/20131010190759/http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0345260597?ie=UTF8&tag=7159-20&linkCode=as2&camp=1789&creative=390957&creativeASIN=0345260597), or a new book which is a sort of sequel to that one.  The trouble is that for some of the most interesting events, including some obscure cons, I’ve forgotten a lot of important details, even such matters as when and where they were held.


Take, for instance the one I think of as the “Johnny Weissmuller Con.”  It was in many ways a great con, with a terrific cast of guests — [**Robert Heinlein**](/fred-pohl/2010-05-03-working-with-robert-a-heinlein/), [**Jack Williamson**](/fred-pohl/2010-08-12-jack-the-wonderful-williamson-part-1-of-many/), [Gordie Dickson](https://web.archive.org/web/20131010190759/http://www.nndb.com/people/729/000023660/)  and many another writer (including me) and even such rarely observed media people as [Johnny Weissmuller](https://web.archive.org/web/20131010190759/http://www.nytimes.com/learning/general/onthisday/bday/0602.html) and Tarzan’s favorite Jane, [Maureen O’Sullivan](https://web.archive.org/web/20131010190759/http://www.farrow-osullivan.com/MaureenOSullivan1.html).


Maureen O’ had been one of my most-loved stars ever since she starred in that early, and pretty sappy, sf film, [Just Imagine](https://web.archive.org/web/20131010190759/http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B001P3XYUE?ie=UTF8&tag=twtfb-20&linkCode=as2&camp=1789&creative=390957&creativeASIN=B001P3XYUE).  Getting a chance to talk to her was pure gravy.  (When you saw the two of them on the stage at the con, it seemed that the personalities of their roles in the [Tarzan movies](https://web.archive.org/web/20131010190759/http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B0001NBLYA?ie=UTF8&tag=twtfb-20&linkCode=as2&camp=1789&creative=390957&creativeASIN=B0001NBLYA) were drawn from the real-life personalities of the pair of them.  Smart, competent Jane had to help musclebound but tongue-tied Tarzan come up with answers to the questions in their on-stage interview.)


So if any of you remember anything about that con, I’d be grateful if you dropped me a line.

I wasn’t there, but I believe you’re referring to what was nicknamed “NoodleCon” after its organizer Robert Nudelman.  I don’t recall now what the convention’s actual name was, or the specific year (70′s or early 80′s, I think).

Nudelman moved to Los Angeles, in part to dodge creditors, and eventually became a respected figure in Hollywood preservation circles there.  He died in 2008.

- [Carol De Priest](https://web.archive.org/web/20131010190759/http://www.tusconfan.com/) says:
Mike is correct. I was there also – it being held in Tucson, AZ, where I live. Around 50 professionals in various fields (authors, media, art, etc.) were there, but you should read David Schow’s article to get an idea of what this convention was like. What Schow fails to mention is that Nudelman was told the convention would fall flat on its face financially due to woefully inadequate advertising, for one reason: it drew fewer than 1000 attendees, according to Schow, although I’d be surprised if there were more than 500. A lot of the guests ended up having to pay out-of-pocket expenses such as hotel rooms and travel, because Nudelman was broke, and his father ended up putting a second mortgage on his house just to meet hotel and convention center fees and such. But many of the guests did enjoy themselves, even if they did have to pay some of the expenses, and it was a heaven for the few fans who came. Many, if not all, were able to approach and talk to pros who, in some cases, were happy to be recognized. Robert Heinlein, unlike his stand-offishness at MidAmeriCon, threw a room party, open to all, and several friends and I spent around an hour there. The drinks were on-the-house, he was in a chair in the center of the room with a friend of mine in his lap, while Ginny looked on with that slightly disapproving look on her face from the couch. I could go on…. but you get the idea. Nudelman thereafter left for Hollywood where he spent the rest of his life (he died young, in May 2008) trying to rescue old Hollywood buildings from the wrecking ball, all the while still living off dear old dad.  

If there’s a moral there, darned if I know what it is.  

But that’s why it’s called NoodleCon, anyway.
