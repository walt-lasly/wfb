- Rich Rostrom says:
Shouldn’t that be “Idiotic Sayings of Bright People” in this case?
Ms. Goldman asserted that obviously false claim because *she* wanted to bring about political changes that couldn’t be accomplished through voting – because most people didn’t want them. Rather than accept this fact, she persuaded herself that the political system was a fraud controlled by the mysterious “they”. This justified the coercive violence which was the only way her program could ever be enacted.
In Ms. Goldman’s universe, political issues would not be settled through the charade of elections, but by the authentic process of violence. The choice of a new public school superintendent or the location of a new highway would be settled in the streets with guns. As Mayakovsky wrote, “Comrade Mauser has the floor.”
But then, Ms. Goldman was *in favor* of murderous political violence, as long as the people who were killed were those she disapproved of.
Goldman’s comment is really the flip side of Mao’s dictum that “Political power grows out of the barrel of a gun.” And we know what Mao’s rule was like. I remember a story Betty told about her visit to to post-Mao China:  *1984* had recently been published there, and some of the Chinese wanted to know how Orwell could have so perfectly foreseen the Greart Proletarian Cultural Revolution.
I’m sorry to be such a curmudgeon about this – but I take serious offense at the snarky glorification of an attitude which caused most of the destruction and suffering of the last century.

- [Stephen](https://web.archive.org/web/20170707103000/http://thisweekatthelibrary.blogspot.com/) says:
I’ve read a fair sampling of Goldman’s work: in  her later years she stopped endorsing violence, as she saw it was problematic at best. It created sympathy for the ATTACKED, and thus ‘propaganda of the deed’ failed utterly.(Red Emma Speaks) Goldman’s view was shared by Henry David Thoreau, who in “On Civil Disobedience” encouraged people morally scandalized by the actions of the government to protest actively. He himself went to jail rather than pay a tax to support the Mexican war, for instance, and he chided those who were content with voting. Howard Zinn believes in the same approach:  voting is too passive. Democracy’s health is ensured by activism, like that demonstrated in the Civil Rights movement.  I think Goldman’s cynicism is well-warranted, given that she was writing in the age of political machines — and remains true today given that politicians are more beholden to their campaign contributors than they are the people who ‘voted’ for them.
