## Frank Herbert, the Dune Man


{{< figure src="herbert-frankbwwb.jpg" alt="Frank Herbert" caption="Frank Herbert" >}}


I met [Frank Herbert](https://web.archive.org/web/20120304202300/http://www.amazon.com/gp/redirect.html?ie=UTF8&location=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.amazon.com%2Fgp%2Fentity%2FFrank-Herbert%2FB000APO5OM%3Fie%3DUTF8%26ref_%3Dsr%5Fntt%5Fsrch%5Flnk%5F1%26qid%3D1277072373%26sr%3D1-1&tag=twtfb-20&linkCode=ur2&camp=1789&creative=390957) and his wife Beverly at the home of [**Poul**](/fred-pohl/2010-06-21-frank-herbert-the-dune-man/) and [Karen](https://web.archive.org/web/20120304202300/http://www.fantasticfiction.co.uk/a/karen-anderson/) Anderson in the early 1960s, where we had all been invited for dinner.  It was a great evening.  There weren’t many people more fun to share a meal with than those four, especially when Karen was creating one of her original recipes (this time with Japanese black beans and I have no idea what else).


We became friendly quickly.  I should mention that the Andersons’ home was in those unexpectedly precipitous hills across the Bay from San Francisco, because when it became going-home time the Herberts and I were driven back to the city by another diner, a local resident who knew every hill and curve and preferred to take them all at high speed while turned halfway around in the driver’s seat in order to have a friendly conversation with us.  When we got out, the Herberts and I agreed that we had just been through a life-changing experience, and we would be lifelong buddies from then on.


Still, we managed to get together only rarely because of problems of geography, except for the occasional fortuitous occasion — for example, the day in the early ’80s, when I was in Seattle on a book tour.  As I was crossing a street on my way to a TV interview, a car pulled up in front of me and a woman stuck her head out the window.  “Hello, sailor,” she called.  “Looking for a good time?”  It was Bev, with Frank grinning over her shoulder from the steering-wheel side.


It wasn’t the best of opportunities for a lengthy chat, but I was glad to see them both looking well; Bev had been diagnosed with early-stage breast cancer and, I knew, was facing surgery. Before the other drivers began honking, the Herberts mentioned that they were building a house in [Hana](https://web.archive.org/web/20120304202300/http://www.hanamaui.com/) on Maui, and I promised that the next time we were in Hawai’i we’d look them up.


Meanwhile Frank, working as a newspaperman, had started to research an article about the [sand dunes of Oregon](https://web.archive.org/web/20120304202300/http://www.fs.fed.us/r6/siuslaw/recreation/tripplanning/oregondunes/index.shtml), and that changed his life.  The dunes fascinated him.  He never finished the article, but he began writing science-fiction stories for [**John Campbell’s Astounding**](/fred-pohl/2009-12-03-astounding-the-campbell-years-riding-high/), starting with a three-part serial about a dune planet and its inhabitants.


Herbert himself thought it might make a pretty good hardcover book but was disappointed by the responses when he tried offering it to publishers.  No book publisher was interested in acquiring the hardcover rights to this rapidly expanding mass of manuscript, however, until an editor at the quite small publishing house of [Chilton Books](https://web.archive.org/web/20120304202300/http://chilton.cengage.com/) managed to stitch the several existing stories into a single huge novel.  He called it [Dune](https://web.archive.org/web/20120304202300/http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0441013597?ie=UTF8&tag=twtfb-20&linkCode=as2&camp=1789&creative=390957&creativeASIN=0441013597), and when he published the result, it became a runaway bestseller, said to be the most profitable sf book ever written.


Frank had written with real people and places in mind, though he gave them invented names for his stories, just as [Cordwainer Smith](https://web.archive.org/web/20120304202300/http://www.webscription.net/chapters/1416521461/1416521461.htm) had for his own stories of the imperfectly concealed Middle East.  Arrakis was Frank Herbert code for Iraq, The Baron was [**Dick Cheney**](/fred-pohl/2009-09-11-dick/), Selusa Secundis was Afghanistan and so on.  (I’m sorry to say that I don’t know all the identities for either author.)
